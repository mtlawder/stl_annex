First Look at State Coronavirus Case numbers (3-29)


There are many different news outlets and academic institutions putting out very informative data, charts, and visuals around the COVID-19 outbreak. I'm not an epidemiologist or infectious disease expert, but with so much different statistics being reported in the news, I wanted to take a look at more of the raw numbers for cases within the United States. In this blog, I'll walk through how I've looked at some of the state level data surrounding COVID-19 at a high level. The analysis below uses The New York Times' County and State level data which they are publishing on github and updating daily. The Times has some awesome visualizations and has been a great resource for news.

Let's start with the raw numbers from each state (we are including DC, Puerto Rico, Virgin Islands and Guam as "states" in the analysis where applicable). By Saturday March 28th, over 28 states had reported over 500 cases (with quiet a few more not far behind) and each one of them has been experiencing sharp growth. The chart below shows the number of cases based on the number of days after the first reported case from each state.

These case numbers do not look good and the sharp upward turn appears pretty unavoidable. They all appear to be trending towards a fairly catastrophic exponential growth. But, as noted elsewhere, it can be difficult to see if the growth is truly exponential or more linear when looking on a linear scale over shorter time periods. In the last week, it has been difficult to track if progress has been made towards slowing the growth rate of cases from social distancing and other measures. Most reports from each state will note the number of new cases (and deaths), but usually just talk about the raw number of new cases. It is important to see, however, if the increase in the new number of daily cases is growing, remaining stagnant, or shrinking. Take New York, the current epicenter, as an example, on 3-22, New York state recorded 4,812 new cases (a 46.5% increase from 3-21) bringing its total cases to 15,168. On 3-28, the state recorded 8,728 new cases to bring its total 53,364. While that raw number is not quiet double the increase from six days prior, it's only a 19.6% increase from the previous day which would indicate that some of the measures for slowing the spread of the virus are working. A big caveat not just with this example, but all of the data, is that case numbers are only as good as the testing occurring (which is to say not great). So we should be careful to put too much stock in trends without putting them into the context of each state. But studying the daily percent change can offer a good window to seeing if daily progress is being made.

Looking at the last ten days, New York, New Jersey, and Illinois appear to be making progress in lowering their percentage growth. But they also started with higher values at the beginning of the period. Again, we are looking at a small time period so, the it's hard to tell if some of the drops are truly trends or just blips (possible caused by changing ability of testing). It can be hard to spot differences from the chart view, so looking at the same data we started with at the top, but in with a log view can be valuable.

Above, log plot of cases based on days since first case in state and below is based on actual date.
There is a slight but consistent downward bend in the state's log plots, which means that measures are having some impact. Regardless of the current number of cases most of the states appear to be showing the same slight downward bend regardless of if they current have 500 or 50,000 cases. Hopefully this early trend will become more significant in coming days and show that the measures taken by all the states are turning back the spread of the virus.
