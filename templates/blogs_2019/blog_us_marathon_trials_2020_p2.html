In Part 1 of my US Marathon Trials Analysis, I looked at the current field that is qualified for the 2020 Trials and how they compared to qualifying fields of the past. Today, we will look at the results from the 04-16 Trials and how individual's results relate to their qualifying times. The above plots contain information on every finisher from the 04-16 Trials (almost, we don't have 10k Qualifiers included, sorry Dan Browne, Blake Russell, and 5 others). Each circle is plotted based on a runner's qualifying time (x-axis) and actual result in the Trials (y-axis). Runners that ran in multiple years will have multiple dots and each Trials cycle is shown with a different color cirlce. Runners with a Full Marathon Qualifying Standard (QS) have a blue border on their circle, while runners with a Half Marathon QS have a red border. Any runners that placed in the top 3 in a race (and qualified for the Olympics) have a gold border instead. If you hover over any circle you can see the runner's name and their times.

For the "All Qualifiers" plot, I converted all Half Qualifier's times into a marathon equivalent based on an equation I empirically derived from the IAAF scoring tables. The exact conversions are for Men: Full time = 2.3117*Half time-625.7s and for Women: Full time = 2.4338*Half time-1306.1s. Looking between the "All Qualifiers" and "Full Marathon QS" options in the plot above gives a good qualitative look at the classic question, "Do Half only qualifiers do as well as Full qualifiers?" A quick look shows not very many Half Qualifiers with the fast finishes in 2016. Although roughly 20% of women used the Half Standard to Qualify for the Trials only 1 women (Janet Bawcom) finished in the top 15 in 2016. On the men's side, Rupp did win as a Half qualifier, but only the only other Half qualifier in the top 15 was Patrick Symth even though a whopping 60% of runners qualified via the Half.

Due to the conditions 2016 is kind of a special case. A huge amount of runners in general and Half qualifiers in particular dropped out in 2016. While 457 runners qualified, only 353 toed the start line in 2016 (233 Marathon QS and 120 Half QS) and 34% of Half QS runners and 29% Full QS runners dropped out. On the men's side, based on converted times lots of the top time qualifiers used the Half Standard to qualify (12 out of the top 20). But only 3 of those 12 finished in less than 2:23 (with 5 dropping out completely). The women's side was very different with only 1 (Bawcom) of the top 20 qualifying marks coming from the Half and she ran well finishing in 5th place.

For the roughly 2/3rds of Half qualifiers that completed the race on both the Men's and Women's sides, they performed to roughly the same level as Full finishers. On the Men's side 34 out of 90 (38%) Half Qualifiers who finished beat their converted Qualifying time while 23 out of 71 (32%) finisher with a Marathon QS beat their time. On the women's side, 7 out of 30 (23%) finishers with a Half QS beat their time while 50 out of 162 (30%)finishers with a Marathon QS beat their time. Based on these numbers, we don't see huge differences in the expected performances of finshers between Half Qualifiers and Full Qualifiers. I should caution extrpolating too much based on 2016 those as conditions for the Trials race were very tough in LA.

More runners have a slower Trials race than their Qualifying standard, so it greatly helps your chances of making a team if you have a very fast qualifying mark (shocking, I know). Not many runners make big leaps at the Trials themselves. Below you can see the qualifying position entering the Trial of each Olympic qualifier (includes converted Half QS and 10k QS from 04 and 08). Only once out of 8 races, did the top seeded runner not advance (2008 Men's race where top seeded 37 yo Khalid Khannuochi finished 4th) and second seeded runners advanced in 7 out of 8 races as well (and honestly it should be 8 for 8 because Patrick Symth's Half Qualifier was rated three seconds better than Meb's 2:09:13 Qualifier and Meb won the race as the "three" seed). But after the top two seeds it becomes a much bigger toss up in terms of who grabs the other Olympic spot.

The third runner to qualify in many of the year does buck the trend and runs close to or faster than their trials mark. In 2016, Jared Ward ran only 4 seconds off his Qualifying time to go from 19th qualifier to the Olympics (although this qualifier number was boosted by a lot of fast Half Qualifiers that year). Similarly in 2012, it was Abdi and in 2008, Ritz, who both ran several minutes faster than their Qualifiers to grab spots. Only the Women's side, Magdalena Lewy Boulet came in with only a 2:42 qualifying mark (good for 29th seed a the start in 2008) before proceeding to obliterate that mark running 2:30 to qualify. And in 2004, it was a similarly spectacular run from Jen Rhines who finshed in 2:29 after qualifying with only a 2:41. So if history says anything, as long as they can make it to the start line, Rupp, Korir, Hasay, and Cragg are heavy favorites for four spots with anybody's guess as to who else gets in.

I could dig in more to alot of the data above (and I encourage you to play around with theplots), but I don't want to end up down too much of a rabbit hole looking at every individual result. So instead, the last area I'll talk about today is seeing how repeat Qualifiers performed. I was hoping to see a clear trend of runners getting better when they ran a second or third trials, such as when Amy Cragg finsihed 4th in 2012 and came back and won the race in 2016. I wanted the numbers to validate the narrative that experience matters, but when we looking at Repeat Qualifier's times, there is just too much variation in race conditions to show individuals getting faster. The line plots below show all runners who finished multiple Trials races and had at least one "good" Trials result (under 2:15 for the Men and under 2:40 for the women) We can tell that in 2016 the race conditions were slow because every single runner who finished the Trials in 2012 and met our time standard ran slower in 2016. On the other hand, 2012 was a faster relative race then 2008, with most runners improving their times (there are a couple exceptions like Ryan Hall, but he qualified for the Olympics both years, so who cares).

When we look at finish times of repeaters in aggregate regardless of year, we see that times actually get slower the more times a repeater runs at the trials. The box and whisker plots show the quantile values for all repeat qualifiers for each time they raced (for example, someone who ran in 2004 and 2008 would have their 2004 time in the "First" category and their 2008 time in the "Second" and someone who raced in 2012 and 2016 would have their 2012 time in the "First" category and 2016 in the "Second"). For the women, there are 84 runners that completed two Trials and 16 that completed three. The average time for these runners in their first Trial's was 2:43:58, the second time they raced the average was 2:45:27 and for the 16 runners that finished a third race the average was 2:48:24. For the three-peaters, I was expecting a faster result mainly because you need to be training at a high level for about a decade to hit three Trials cycles. But among the three-peaters, in addition to the Olympians Deena Kastor and Des Linden, we have a sizable group of very consistent runners that were able to qualify three times with times just under the Standard. Meghan Arbogast might be the best example for a three-peater with consistent times over a decade qualifying in 2004 with a 2:45:57, coming back in 2008 with a 2:45:46, and again qualifying in 2012 with a 2:45:48. Even when we throw away the slow 2016 results, we still see a similar trend. So I guess the takeaway message is if you just qualified for your first Trials, don't waste your chance because you might not get faster!
