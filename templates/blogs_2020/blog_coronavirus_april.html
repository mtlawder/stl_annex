Coronavirus in the US - Metros, County, and States (Metros only for now)


At the end of March, I put up a post looking at the very early trend of State's trend away from exponential growth as stay-at-home orders and social distancing across the nation were starting to show some impact. That trend of slowing the growth rate has continued and I put up a dashboard showcasing the statistics which I've been regularly updating. Now, the country has been continuing to contemplate if we are reaching the peak of cases and death. Some models show some states are well past their peaks, with others peak still more than a week away. A major takeaway here is that while the US as a whole appears around its peak right now, different states and individual counties will vary by days and weeks in when they peak and when they "complete" their curve. We will take a more granular look comparing cases and deaths across several states, counties, and metro areas, to see how there curves compare. Additionally, we should not forget that it's also very important to look at how high the peak for cases and deaths reaches.

Metropolitan Areas

First let's look at the Metros. In terms of raw cases, the urban and suburban areas of cities have many cases (unsurprising since they also have, you know, many people). But as with states or counties, each metro area has different rules and restrictions in place and have seen a wide range of different cases loads and deaths. Looking at metro areas (defined by the census bureau as a Metropolitan Statistic Area) that have over 2.5M, we see a wide range in the number of cases, but similar trends in terms of recent growth rates. There are 24 Metros with over 2.5M people based on the census bureau's 2019 population estimates. When combined, these Metros contain a little over 40% of the US' total population or 135M people (and account for 52% of virus cases). Notable absent from this group in New Orleans, which has seen a large amount of cases, but only has 1.3M citizens.

To look at how different metros have fared, let's look at each region's rolling 5-day % change in cases. Below, you can see how in late March most metros were seeing cases more than double every 5 days (growth rate over 100%), but by yesterday (4-13) all 5-day growth rates were below 50%, with many under under 20%. Note that these are for new cases (not active cases) and therefore do not account for recoveries. Slide the bar below to check out how the growth rates have changed.

Several Metros have seen higher case total even as they have driven down growth rates recently. New York has seen by far the most total cases (139k), while Chicago, Detroit, and Boston are next up with all around 20 thousand positive cases. While case numbers provide a lens into the outbreaks, they can be distorted by the amount of testing actually occurring in the metros. Since I won't dig into any testing numbers in this analysis, the case numbers will have to do. Since measures have been taken across the country in the last month, metros have avoided worst case scenarios, but how much worse would the case numbers be if all metros cases looked more like New York's. Total cases through 4-13 for the 24 metros compared here was 303k cases. However, if all metros had the same rate as New York city (67 cases per 10,000 people), total cases would be almost 3x as high at over 910k cases. On the other hand, if case rates were at the levels seen in Minneapolis (the lowest rate among major metros at 2.9 cases per 10,000 people), cases would be a a relatively meager 39k cases across all 24 metros, less than a third of what New York alone has seen.

Since we know testing differs across these metros, I compared the per capita deaths with the per capita positive tests to try to get a sense of which metros are testing more with the assumption being that number of deaths may be a better indicator of the level of an outbreak than cases. But reported deaths may not be as accurate as we would like right now, so take the analysis with a grain of salt. Other factors go into the relationship between how many people that test positive will die from Coronavirus like access to hospitals, but in general as cases per capita increase, I would expect deaths per capita to increase. Across all 24 metros for every 1,000 positive cases there have been 34.5 deaths. Metros with a ratio higher than this number may not be testing as extensively as other locales. Metros that stand out as having less deaths per positive case are Orlando, Charlotte, and Houston which so far each have less than 15 deaths per 1,000. But each of these metros have had relatively small outbreaks with less than 10 positive cases for every 10,000 residents. When looking at metros that have had at least 20 cases per 10,000 residents, Philadelphia and Miami have had some of the lowest death per case ratios at 24 and 23, respectively. On the flip side, Detroit has stood out as a major outlier showing 66 deaths per 1,000 cases while New York and Chicago, both with relatively large outbreaks have been closer to the metro average, both at 36 deaths per 1,000 test cases.
