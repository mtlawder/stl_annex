Coronavirus in New Jersey - Numbers by County


New Jersey is currently firmed entrenched as the state with the second most COVID-19 cases after New York, pushing past 100k confirmed cases this past week. While New Jersey seems to have halted the growth of new daily cases, they have yet to see large drops in new cases like New York has been experience over the last week. New Jersey's new reported cases have averaged around 3,500 new cases per day for the last three weeks. While New Jersey as a whole has the highest population density of any state in the US, the state has a wide variety of urban, suburban, and rural counties. With case numbers unchanged for so long at a state level, I wanted to look closer at the county level to see if individual county trends have mirrored the state level numbers.


New Jersey has 21 counties. Hudson county which sits across the Hudson river from New York City has a population density of over 14,000 residents per square mile, while Salem county along the southern boarder of the state has 185 residents per square mile. Between those two, the other nineteen counties cover all types of densities, land-uses, sizes, and shapes. Using The New York Times compiled county case and death numbers, I'll look through how these counties vary in how they have experienced the spread of COVID-19.

When looking at the raw number of cases by county, 3 different groupings of counties began to emerge after April 1, and have remained relatively consistent in terms of new daily cases for almost the entire month. The groups are listed below and you can check out the cases numbers in the charts and map below. The highest case totals come from the 6 counties closest to New York City and with the 6 highest population densities. All 6 counties in Group 1 have between 300 and 600 new cases per day. Group 2 counties each have between 100-200 new cases per day and comprise the seven counties from the remaining suburban areas between New York and Philadelphia along with the Northern Shore counties. Group 3, with 8 counties that all have below 50 new cases each day, comprises of the five most southern Jersey counties along with the three more rural counties of Sussex, Warren, and Hunterdon in the North.

While these counties show some clear separation when looking at raw cases, when instead we look at new cases per capita, the groups begin to blur. For example, looking at Bergen county from G1, Ocean county from G2, and Warren county from G3, their avg new daily cases for 4/21-4/23 are 346, 192, and 35, respectively. But when adjusting for population, those values become 37.1 (Bergen), 31.6 (Ocean), and 33.2 (Warren) new cases per 100k resident. There is still a wide gap between the 6.9/100k new cases in Cap May and the 92.6/100k new cases in Passaic, but there are not as clear groupings across the board. And while there has been some mild success for a few counties lowering their new cases from peaks in early May, most counties trends appear to mirror the state as a whole plateauing over the last several weeks. Some of the counties that have made progress are Bergen (-54% below peak), Essex (-31%), Morris (-38%), and Monmouth (-50%), that progress has appeared to stalled or at least slowed in the past two weeks.

I haven't analyzed detailed testing numbers, but the New Jersey Health department reports the overall positive test rate to be 44% across the state. While this rate is similar to New York's positive test rate, it's well above the National average of 18% positive and one of the highest positive testing rates of any state. With such a high percentage of positive tests, it would appear that New Jersey should be testing more (compared to other parts of the country) and that the number of cases in official numbers may be difficult to compare apples-to-apples with other states. But these are the numbers that are available, so with that caveat in place let's compare some of New Jersey's counties to other parts of the country.

I looked at all counties across the county that had at some point reported at least 50 new daily cases (3 day avg) which includes all 13 counties in Group 1 and 2 from New Jersey. In addition to the 13 NJ counties, there are 130 more counties to hit this threshold spread across 36 states (and D.C.). I'll note here that we are excluding NYC counties because The New York Times data set reports them as a single entity, so New York, Richmond, Queens, Kings, and Bronx Counties are not included. When each of these counties hit their peak of new daily cases ranges from late March up to the most recent day of data (4/23). There are 28 counties who peak was 4/23, 4/22, or 4/21 and are presumably still seeing case numbers grow at an increasing rate or just peaking. Camden, Hudson, Mercer, and Burlington counties are each part of that group with all four of their peaks coming on 4/21. Other notable areas with stubbornly rising cases include Massachusetts (4 counties with peaks on 4/23 including Suffolk [Boston]), Los Angeles county which had a peak of 1365 cases on 4/22, and Cook county (Chicago) in Illinois which saw 1237 new cases on 4/23. For this group of counties, they are at best plateauing and possibly still on the upswing of case loads. Most look similar to Hudson county which is a great example of plateauing county. The county hit 551 new cases on 4/5 and 2.5 weeks later on 4/21 had 560 cases with some modest fluctuations in between.

Excluding these 28 counties, I ranked the remaining 115 counties by the percentage drop in new cases since their peak. The rankings are not measuring how bad a county was hit with cases, but looks at how well have they been able to reduce cases since peaking. A county which had more cases early in the pandemic in theory has more room to lower those case totals. Below is a table of the remaining 9 NJ counties rankings and percentage. The results were mixed for New Jersey. Bergen was the best performer ranking 39th in percentage drop cutting its new cases by more than half since it peak in early April. Monmouth was the only other county ranked in the top half of counties. The rest fared worse than average with Passaic's drop of only 9.4% ranking 112th (4th worst). New Jersey as a whole continues to struggle to drive down new cases, and after looking deeper at the county level, the same plateauing trend since in the state numbers is mirrored in in most counties. Whether hit with 500 or 50 cases a day, counties numbers have remained mostly stagnant for the last 2 weeks. A few of the bright spots have come from counties with the most cumulative cases where Bergen, Essex and Union county have at least dropped over 30% or more off of their peaks. Hopefully more of the state can build off the small successes seen so far and new cases can start dropping more rapidly as many other counties across the nation have.
